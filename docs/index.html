The paper examined the emergence and early spread of the SARS-CoV-2 variant BA.3.2, first identified in South Africa in late 2024, with a focus on its detection within the United States and globally. Researchers employed a multimodal genomic surveillance strategy incorporating traveler-based sampling, clinical specimens, airplane wastewater, and community wastewater monitoring across multiple states. BA.3.2 carries a substantial number of substitutions and deletions in the spike protein relative to the antigens used in current COVID-19 vaccines, raising concern that existing immune protection may be reduced. Surveillance detected the variant in travelers, clinical patients, and wastewater systems spanning dozens of states, with the variant also reported in more than twenty countries worldwide. The authors conclude that continued genomic surveillance is essential given BA.3.2's potential to evade vaccine- and infection-derived immunity and its ongoing public health implications.

The multiply iterated law of the iterated logarithm: game-theoretic foundations of sequential detection boundaries

Akshay Balsubramani. The multiply iterated law of the iterated logarithm: game-theoretic foundations of sequential detection boundaries. arXiv. 2026 preprint

This paper investigates the theoretical foundations of anytime-valid confidence sequences and e-processes, which are widely used tools for sequential hypothesis testing and detection. The authors reformulate the standard recipe of mixing exponential test statistics over a prior as a two-player zero-sum game in which a Learner chooses a prior and Nature adaptively constructs a difficult score process, with a pathwise Gibbs-variational identity governing the Learner's wealth along every realized path. From this game-theoretic framework, they derive that the law of the iterated logarithm emerges as the minimax detection boundary rather than as an artifact of combinatorial slack, and that the optimal prior is not a free design choice but is uniquely determined as the equalizer strategy that renders every boundary-crossing time equally costly for Nature. This equalizer strategy turns out to coincide with the Jeffreys prior on the scale-of-scales in log-log coordinates, and it yields a sharp closed-form correction to the iterated logarithm boundary with an explicitly characterized coefficient. The paper unifies a range of previously disparate constructions—including Howard-Ramdas stitching, betting confidence sequences, and the Erdős-Kolmogorov integral test—as special cases of this single equalizer principle, with an empirical companion evaluation confirming the central identities.

The Role of Scintillation in Detecting HI Absorption in FRB Spectra

Om Gupta, Pawan Kumar, Paz Beniamini et al.. The Role of Scintillation in Detecting HI Absorption in FRB Spectra. arXiv. 2026 preprint

This paper investigates whether the neutral hydrogen 21-cm absorption line, long predicted to appear in Fast Radio Burst spectra, can realistically be detected given the complicating effects of scintillation. The authors developed an efficient simulation model of diffractive scintillation from a thin scattering screen to characterize how frequency-dependent flux modulations interact with the absorption profile across different signal regimes. They found that detection is feasible when the scintillation decorrelation bandwidth differs substantially in scale from the absorption line width, and that stacking repeated bursts can improve detectability provided the bursts are separated in time by more than the diffractive scintillation timescale to avoid correlated frequency modulations. Cross-referencing known repeating FRB positions with a catalog of Milky Way molecular clouds suggests one particular repeating source may intersect a Galactic molecular cloud. Even with current and planned sensitive telescopes, however, the combined effects of scintillation and detector noise require stacking a very large number of bursts to achieve a statistically significant detection, with improved detector sensitivity being key to eventually probing hydrogen clouds in host galaxies, intervening galaxies, or high-redshift minihalos.

Kinematic detection of dusty outflows from AGN: PAH kinematics of type 2 quasars with JWST/MIRI spectroscopy

Fergus R. Donnan, Cristina Ramos Almeida, Omaira González Martín et al.. Kinematic detection of dusty outflows from AGN: PAH kinematics of type 2 quasars with JWST/MIRI spectroscopy. arXiv. 2026 preprint

This paper investigated the kinematics of dust in the outflows of type 2 quasars by analyzing Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbon features observed with JWST/MIRI spectroscopy. The researchers applied Principal Component Analysis tomography to produce velocity maps of PAH emission in five type 2 quasars drawn from the Quasar Feedback sample, comparing the dust kinematics with those of ionized and molecular gas tracers. Velocity maps of the large, neutral PAH feature at 11.3 microns were successfully produced for three of the five objects, all of which showed clear evidence of dusty outflows after subtracting disk kinematics, with molecular hydrogen rotational transitions providing consistent supporting evidence. The ionized PAH feature at 6.2 microns could not be mapped, possibly due to intrinsic profile differences or AGN-driven suppression, consistent with prior findings that AGN outflows preferentially contain neutral rather than ionized PAHs. The results suggest that dusty outflows become more prevalent at higher Eddington ratios and may play an important role in AGN evolution, though the authors caution that the small sample size limits strong conclusions.

CacheMPC: Certified Cached Model Predictive Control for Quadruped Locomotion

Nimesh Khandelwal, Mehul Anand, Shakti S. Gupta et al.. CacheMPC: Certified Cached Model Predictive Control for Quadruped Locomotion. arXiv. 2026 preprint

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in Model Predictive Control for quadruped locomotion, where solving quadratic programs each control cycle limits update rates on embedded hardware. The proposed Certified CacheMPC system stores and reuses previously computed contact-force trajectory solutions using a locality-sensitive hashing scheme, retrieving cached solutions only when a posterior certificate confirms feasibility and bounds cost suboptimality, with a fallback hierarchy that combines cached retrieval, deadline-bounded fresh solves, and shifted prior solutions. The framework was evaluated extensively in MuJoCo simulation and deployed on physical hardware, achieving large median speedups in solve time in both simulation and on-robot settings. Stability testing across thousands of trials found no statistically significant difference in closed-loop stable rates between cache variants and the baseline, though the specific safety contribution of the certificate mechanism could not be resolved at the tested sample sizes.
